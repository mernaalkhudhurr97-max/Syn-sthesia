Part 1: Project Direction
Working Title: Living Score

Our team has decided to create an original interactive audiovisual piece.

Team Vision 
Living Score is an original interactive audiovisual piece which creates an influence between sounds and visuals. Unlike the current music visualisers we have seen online, our project connects the artwork wth music.By combining a WAV soundtrack with MIDI data to generate drum beats, piano melodies, guitar notes, and bass rhythms, etc. These musical output triggers dynamic visual responses like visual movement, colour shifts, and animated effects, while selected visual elements will also be capable of controlling the music.

Our inspiration is based on Patt Vira’s generative projects like Rainbow Pendulum Waves and Musical Onion in which color, rhythm and movemnets are being connected by the written codes.We’re also inspired by abstract audio visualisers that use circles, waves, pulses, and particles to express musical energy. We aim to build develop a system to let users feel connected while balancing all the music, visuals and other components in our project.

Mechanic 1: Audio / Music Output System

Owner: Jake

The audio mechanic is responsible for combining a WAV soundtrack with multiple MIDI files to produce musical triggers that drive the rest of the project. While the WAV file delivers the main audio experience for the audience, the MIDI data provides precise timing and note information for sounds such as kick drums, snares, hi-hats, piano notes, guitar melodies, and bass lines. By doing so, the system will be able to identify musical events accurately without depending only on the FFT analysis. MIDI supports both single-note triggers and multiple simultaneous notes, enabling more interaction between sound and visuals.

This mechanic also reinforces the project’s concept of a relationship between music and visuals by allowing certain visual elements to control audio behaviour by muting, switching, or adjusting the volume of different sound layers. FFT analysis of the final audio output will also influence other visual aspects like movement, colour, and atmosphere. This person is critical as it is the "music brain" of the project which allows ther mechanics to further work on the project and provides direction for the project. 




Mechanic 2: Random Animation

Owner: Hayley 

This mechanic is responsible for generating animated visual elements that move in dynamic and unpredictable ways across the canvas. Random values are used to control properties like the position, size, speed, colour, direction, and overall behaviour, allowing elements like shapes and lines to appear and interact differently every time the project runs. Some of these visual elements will also respond to musical triggers, such as expanding when a kick drum occurs or shifting direction in response to a snare hit. 

This mechanic supports the project by making the artwork feel lively and constantly evolving rather than in fixed or repetitive patterns. Although the movements are driven by randomness, they remain connected with the audio system so the visuals still synchronise with the music. Although users may not have direct control over every animated element, they will experience continuous motion, variation, and energy throughout the piece.




Mechanic 3: Background Visual

Owner: Merna

The background visual mechanic is responsible for creating the atmosphere and emotional tone of the project. Rather than using a static background colour, the system will feature animations like flowing waves, particles, and Perlin noise textures that react to the music in real time. The involved FFT frequency data will influence the behaviour in the background, where lower frequencies might generate slow pulsing movements and higher frequencies can produce brighter flashes, sharper flickers, or particle effects. The appearance of the background also shifts depending on the active musical layer, like drums, bass, piano, or guitar, allowing the visual environment to change along with the soundtrack. 

This mechanic helps unify the entire artwork by connecting the audio system and animated elements into a cohesive experience visually for audiences.




Mechanic 4: Visual Control of Audio Effects

Owner: Team Member 4

This mechanic allows visual elements in the artwork to influence the audio system directly , creating an interaction between sounds and visuals. Rather than having music only control animation, certain shapes and objects on the canvas will also affect the sound behaviour through their movement, position, size, or interaction. For example, an object moving across the screen may gradually increase or decrease based on the volume of the bass, piano, or drum layers and entering a specific area could mute, unmute, or switch particular sounds. Certain visual elements may also trigger simple audio effects or transitions. The user interaction through mouse or keyboard inputs can therefore further influence the visual controllers, letting the audience to shape the music in real time.

This mechanic supports the project vision by establishing a feedback relationship between images and sounds, making the artwork feel more immersive, interactive, and performative rather than functioning as a traditional one-directional music visualiser.





Part 3: Putting It Together

The final project acts as a fully integrated audiovisual system where sound and visuals operate in continuous interaction with each other. A WAV file provides a primary audio experience and MIDI data supplies precise musical triggers that identify events like drum hits, melodic notes, and rhythmic changes. These triggers drive both the random animation system and the background visuals, making sure that the movement, colour, and form are directly influenced by the music.
In addition, the selected visual elements are capable of affecting the audio through muting, switching, or making adjustments on the different sound layers, reinforcing a closely connected relationship between the components. The FFT analysis of the final audio functions as an additional layer of responsiveness which allows visuals to react not only to the original musical data but also to the sound as it has been modified through interaction. Overall, this creates a unified and evolving piece of work where music and visuals continuously shape one another in real time.
